# Persona Interview: Ms. Deepa (Follow-Up) — The "Smart Recess" Positioning

## Overview

**Interview Date:** 2026-02-24  
**Persona:** Ms. Deepa — School Teacher (Grade 1-2)  
**Context:** Follow-up to test "hybrid positioning" — playground activities with visibility, not curriculum alignment  
**Goal:** Validate whether "smart recess" (activity tracking without standards) has value to teachers

---

## Interview Transcript

### Setting the Context

**Interviewer:** Ms. Deepa, thanks for speaking again. Last time we talked about curriculum alignment, but I want to explore a different angle. What if the app isn't trying to *teach* your curriculum at all — what if it's just a "smart recess" tool that gets kids moving and creating, but shows you what they did?

**Ms. Deepa:** [Pauses, intrigued] "Smart recess"... okay, that's different. Go on.

**Interviewer:** Same activities — tracing, drawing, building, moving — but no "Grade 2 standards" or lesson plans. Kids choose what to explore. You just get a simple log: "Kabir did 15 minutes of air drawing today." Not "mastered letter A." Would that be useful to you, or worthless?

**Ms. Deepa:** [Thinks] Hmm. That's... actually interesting. Let me think about my day.

---

### The Recess Reality

**Ms. Deepa:** Here's my problem — we have 40-minute periods. By minute 30, half my class is squirming. They NEED to move. But I can't just say "go play" — parents complain that I'm "wasting time."

If I could say: *"We did 10 minutes of active play — here's what Kabir chose to do"* — that's documentation. Not curriculum, but proof of engagement.

**Finding #1: "Smart Recess" Has Value as Documentation for Parents**
- **Severity:** MEDIUM-HIGH
- **Evidence:** Teachers need to justify "non-academic" time to parents; simple activity logs provide cover
- **Reframe:** Not "learning outcomes" but "engagement evidence"
- **Recommendation:** Position as "active break documentation" — "Your child chose to draw with their hands for 12 minutes today"

---

### Minimum Viable Data

**Interviewer:** What minimum data would you actually need? Forget rubrics and standards.

**Ms. Deepa:** [Counts on fingers]

1. **"What did they do?"** — "Tracing," "Drawing," "Moving" — categories, not curriculum codes
2. **"For how long?"** — "15 minutes active play" — that's it
3. **"Did they engage?"** — Not "mastery" but "tried 3 different activities"

I DON'T need:
- Letter grades or "proficiency levels"
- Comparison to other kids
- "Grade level" alignment

**Finding #2: Teachers Need "Engagement Tracking" Not "Learning Assessment"**
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Evidence:** Ms. Deepa wants simple activity logs: category, duration, variety
- **Reframe:** From "assessment dashboard" to "activity journal"
- **Recommendation:** 
  - Simple format: "Kabir explored: Tracing (8 min), Free Draw (7 min)"
  - No percentages, no "mastery" labels
  - Weekly summary: "5 active sessions this week"

---

### Parent Communication Test

**Interviewer:** Parents ask "What did my child learn today?" How would you answer with this app?

**Ms. Deepa:** [Practices] Instead of "We worked on phonics" — which is curriculum language — I'd say:

> "Kabir had 15 minutes of free choice time. He chose to draw letters in the air with his hands — that's fine motor practice disguised as play. Then he built a virtual tower for 10 minutes — spatial reasoning. No formal lesson, but lots of engagement."

The app just gives me the FACTS: "Did 15 min of air tracing." I translate for parents. I don't need the app to call it "learning."

**Finding #3: Teachers Want "Raw Activity Data" to Frame Their Own Way**
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Evidence:** Ms. Deepa prefers simple facts she can interpret vs. pre-labeled "learning outcomes"
- **Reframe:** Provide activity logs; let teachers/parents apply their own meaning
- **Recommendation:**
  - Export: "Activity Log" not "Progress Report"
  - Neutral language: "Explored" not "Learned"
  - Parent-friendly but not preachy: "Kabir moved his body for 20 minutes today"

---

### The "Free Choice" Test

**Interviewer:** What if kids can do ANY activity in any order? No "complete Level 1 to unlock Level 2"?

**Ms. Deepa:** [Surprised] That's... actually better for my classroom. Here's why:

In my class of 35, kids finish worksheets at different speeds. Fast finishers get bored and disrupt. If they could CHOOSE to "go play" in the app — trace, draw, move — without me managing it, that's classroom management gold.

The "unlock" system is actually a pain. I have to remember who unlocked what. Free choice means I say: "Done with work? Go explore for 10 minutes." Zero management from me.

**Finding #4: "Open Playground" Model Reduces Teacher Management Burden**
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Evidence:** Ms. Deepa prefers free choice over gated progression; easier for classroom logistics
- **Reframe:** "No curriculum" = "No lesson planning required"
- **Recommendation:** 
  - Market to teachers: "Zero prep. Kids choose. You get activity logs."
  - Emphasize: "No lesson planning, no assignments, no grading"
  - Position as: "Independent activity station"

---

### Comparison to GoNoodle / YouTube Kids

**Interviewer:** What do you use now for active breaks?

**Ms. Deepa:** GoNoodle. But it's passive — they watch a video and dance along. The kids like it, but they're not CREATING anything. And I get zero data. Parents ask "What did they do?" and I say "We danced." That's it.

Your app with hand tracking — kids are DOING, not watching. AND I get a log. That's better than GoNoodle for me.

YouTube Kids? [Makes a face] I don't trust it. Random videos, ads, weird content. If your app is "safe creative play with visibility," I'd prefer that.

**Finding #5: "Smart Recess" Positioning Differentiates from GoNoodle/YouTube**
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Evidence:** Current tools are passive (GoNoodle) or unsafe (YouTube); active creation + visibility is unique
- **Market Position:** "GoNoodle but kids create, not just consume"
- **Recommendation:**
  - Competitive positioning: "Not a video. Not a curriculum. Active creation with proof of play."
  - Target: Schools using GoNoodle for brain breaks

---

### The "Recess Report" Feature Test

**Interviewer:** Imagine a weekly "Recess Report" you send to parents. What would it say?

**Ms. Deepa:** [Designs it mentally]

---
**Kabir's Active Play This Week**
- Tracing in the air: 3 sessions, 35 minutes total
- Free drawing: 2 sessions, 20 minutes total  
- Movement games: 1 session, 15 minutes total
- **Total active time: 70 minutes**

*No formal lessons — just child-directed exploration and movement.*

---

That's it. No "grades," no "levels," no "behind/ahead." Just: "Your child moved, created, and explored for 70 minutes this week."

Some parents will complain: "But what did they LEARN?" And I'll say: "They practiced fine motor skills, spatial reasoning, and self-directed play. That's developmentally appropriate for 7-year-olds."

**Finding #6: "Recess Report" Format — Simple, Neutral, Parent-Defensible**
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Evidence:** Ms. Deepa designed ideal report: activity categories + time + total; no learning labels
- **Reframe:** From "progress report" to "activity summary"
- **Recommendation:** 
  - Weekly "Recess Report" template:
    - Activity categories with icons
    - Total time per category
    - Weekly total
    - Disclaimer: "Child-directed exploration, not formal instruction"

---

### The Pushback Test

**Interviewer:** What would principals or parents push back on with this "smart recess" positioning?

**Ms. Deepa:** [Thinks hard]

**From principals:** "Is this instructional time or wasted time?"
- My defense: "Research says movement improves focus. This is active brain break with documentation."

**From parents:** "Why isn't my child learning letters?"
- My defense: "They ARE practicing letters — but through play, not drill. See? 35 minutes of air tracing this week."

**From curriculum coordinators:** "Does this align with our scope and sequence?"
- [Pauses] This is the tricky one. If they NEED standards alignment, this positioning doesn't work.

But... [brightens]... if I frame it as "recess enrichment" not "curriculum supplement," curriculum coordinators don't care. Recess doesn't need alignment.

**Finding #7: Positioning as "Recess Enrichment" Bypasses Curriculum Scrutiny**
- **Severity:** HIGH (Strategic insight)
- **Evidence:** Curriculum coordinators only care about "instructional time"; "recess" is exempt
- **Reframe:** Don't compete with curriculum apps — complement them as "active breaks"
- **Recommendation:**
  - Category: "Recess Technology" not "Educational Software"
  - Sell to: PE teachers, classroom teachers for brain breaks, not curriculum directors
  - Pricing: "Recess budget" not "instructional materials budget"

---

### Final Verdict

**Interviewer:** Bottom line: Is "smart recess with visibility" valuable to you, or do you need actual curriculum alignment?

**Ms. Deepa:** [Definitive] For my purposes — managing a classroom of 35 kids and justifying time to parents — "smart recess with visibility" is MORE useful than another curriculum app.

I already HAVE curriculum. I need:
- Kids to stop squirming
- Parents to stop complaining about "wasted time"
- Zero prep for me

Your app does all three IF you give me simple activity logs. I don't need it to teach my curriculum. I need it to keep kids active while I get paperwork done.

**But** — [caution] — this only works if parents accept "play is valuable." Some parents want worksheets. They won't like this positioning.

**Finding #8: "Smart Recess" Viable for Teachers, Risky with Some Parents**
- **Severity:** HIGH (Market positioning insight)
- **Evidence:** Ms. Deepa prefers "smart recess" over curriculum app; zero prep, classroom management benefit
- **Caveat:** Some parents want traditional "learning"; may reject play-based framing
- **Recommendation:**
  - **Teacher pitch:** "Active breaks with zero prep and parent documentation"
  - **Parent pitch:** "Playful practice of motor skills and creativity" (not "just play")
  - **Avoid:** "Educational curriculum" — sets wrong expectations
